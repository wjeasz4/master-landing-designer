# Examples

## Purpose

`examples/` holds annotated reasoning traces. An example shows how evidence, constraints, alternatives, and trade-offs lead to a decision—and how a different context could produce a different result.

Examples teach transfer, not imitation.

## What Belongs Here

- product and audience evidence relevant to a bounded decision;
- options that were genuinely considered;
- complete or excerpted Decision Records;
- before-and-after reasoning where the change in judgment is explicit;
- review findings traced back to underlying decisions;
- examples of rejecting a popular pattern for contextual reasons; and
- counterexamples that reveal the limit of a principle.

## What Does Not Belong Here

- demo landing pages;
- screenshot galleries without analysis;
- copy-ready layouts, copy, or code;
- “best-in-class” collections based on taste;
- disguised imitation of an existing product;
- outcomes without alternatives and rationale; or
- examples selected only because they look impressive.

## Required Example Structure

Every example should make these elements visible:

1. product and audience context;
2. evidence and its strength;
3. the decision problem;
4. options considered;
5. the selected direction and rationale;
6. trade-offs and risks;
7. relevant constitutional principles;
8. review findings; and
9. what would change the decision in another context.

Redact or fictionalize confidential information without weakening the reasoning. Label constructed examples clearly.

## How Codex Should Use This Collection

Load examples to understand a reasoning pattern, not to borrow an expression. Compare the example's context with the current task. State the meaningful differences before reusing any heuristic.

If an example becomes a visual target, stop and return to product evidence, [knowledge](../knowledge/index.md), and [patterns](../patterns/index.md).

## Contribution Standard

A contribution must:

- comply with the [Repository Constitution](../CONSTITUTION.md);
- expose reasoning and rejected alternatives;
- identify evidence, inference, and assumptions;
- include trade-offs and review consequences;
- avoid proprietary or unlicensed material;
- explain transfer limits; and
- increase reusable Design Intelligence rather than repository volume.

## Relationship to the System

Examples connect abstract principles to observable judgment. They may reference [Decision Record templates](../templates/index.md), [review checklists](../checklists/index.md), and authoritative [references](../references/index.md), but they never override them.
